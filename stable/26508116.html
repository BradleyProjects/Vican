Abstract

Major social-media companies profess liberal values like global community and democratic participation as primary incentives motivating the design of their platforms and their proposed solutions to online disinformation. However, the industry of digital marketing that underlies these companies’ business models is in tension with these values. Digital marketing ascribes more value to users from demographics with higher socioeconomic status, and online disinformation campaigns that harness the infrastructure of digital advertising built into social media undermine democratic participation. Regulators and the public have called on social-media companies to address the global rise of online disinformation, emphasizing the detection and removal of foreign actors, particularly Russian hackers. This essay argues that the contentious narrative of foreign actors in online disinformation distracts from the foundational role of digital marketing in driving the spread of disinformation online with implications for the design of effective regulation.
